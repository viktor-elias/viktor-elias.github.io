2020 U.S. Presidential Election: A Story of Russian Disinformation

Viktor Elias


Since the 2016 U.S. presidential election, between Donald Trump and Hillary Clinton, there has been quite a lot of talk of Russian efforts to influence American elections, but in 2020, the story is more complex. In contrast to 2016, the media in 2020 has avoided spinning webs of conspiracies to pen the Russians for the shocking election result that led Trump to become President Trump. In a three month period, between Sept. 14 and Nov. 20, I analyzed stories from reputable news outlets to get to the bottom of the Russia collusion story, as outlined below.


Sept. 14 to Sept. 20

On Sept. 15, USA Today reported that Bob Woodward wrote that former National Intelligence Director Dan Coats had thought it was useless to speak out against Trump. Woodward also wrote, in his new book, "Rage," that Coats believed Trump had no moral compass and that he was beholden to Putin.

On Sept. 16, The New York Times reported that Trump had rejected professional scientific conclusions of his own government about the prospects for a widely available coronavirus vaccine and the effectiveness of masks in curbing the spread of the virus as the death toll neared 200,000. Although The Times' reporting at the time did not mention how some of the conspiracies Trump was pushing on Twitter and elsewhere came straight from Russian propaganda, it is clear there is an undertone in the article about where this information was coming from.

On Sept. 17, Politico and other outlets reported that FBI Director Christopher Wray described "very active efforts" by Russia to interfere in the 2020 election, through efforts to damage former Vice President and now President-elect Joseph Biden. Wray is said to have warned that Russians have been using social media and "proxies," such as state media, to hurt Biden and what it views as anti-Russian factions.

On Sept. 17, The Daily Beast broke a story that alleged that for a year and a half, U.S. intelligence officials had warned Trump officials that Rudy Giuliani's close friend, Andriy Derkach, was engaged in suspected election interference. Officials inside the national security apparatus had drafted numerous reports warning that Russian proxies, such as Derkach, were attempting to undermine the 2020 election process by spreading wild conspiracy theories about the integrity of the system.

On Sept. 17, Coats published an op-ed in The New York Times. He suggests that Congress creates an independent commission to oversee the 2020 election and denounced efforts to undermine Americans' confidence in the legitimacy of the election, because "democracy's enemies, foreign and domestic," were trying to chip away at its foundation. "If we fail to take every conceivable effort to ensure the integrity of our election, the winners will not be Donald Trump or Joe Biden, Republicans or Democrats," he adds. "The only winners will be Vladimir Putin, Xi Jinping and Ali Khamenei."

Sept. 21 to Sept. 27


On Sept. 22, The New York Times and other outlets reported that Putin is likely continuing to approve and personally direct interference operations aimed at raising Trump's re-election chances. The Times' reporting was based on a recent C.I.A. analysis that they obtained, adding, "The C.I.A. has moderate confidence in its analysis, a lower degree of certainty than its 2016 assessment of Mr. Putin's preferences, in part because the intelligence community appears to lack intercepted communications or other direct evidence confirming his direction of Mr. Derkach's efforts."


On Sept. 22, The New York Times reported that Trump was feeding many of the Russian disinformation campaigns aiming to improve his re-election chances. "During that time, the [national security apparatus] said, hackers could amplify 'disinformation that includes reports of voter suppression, cyberattacks targeting election infrastructure, voter or ballot fraud and other problems intended to convince the public of the elections’ illegitimacy," the reporting said.


On Sept. 22, The Washington Post and other outlets reported that Facebook removed several fake accounts and pages operated out of China that promoted and criticized both Trump and Biden, in the company's first takedown of Chinese accounts aimed at U.S. politics. Facebook said the roughly 10 Chinese accounts and five pages were part of a broader takedown as Facebook enforces its policy against "coordinated inauthentic behavior (sic)" and interference by foreign governments.


On Sept. 24, NBC News reported that Tyler Technologies, a major software supplier to U.S. city and county governments, had been hacked. Although the company did not directly work in elections software, its products can be used to post information about results and polling place locations, NBC suggested. A couple days later, The New York Times broke a story alleging that the company was conducted by a Russian criminal group that had tied to the KGB and that it had the hallmarks of a Russian state-sanctioned hack.


On Sept. 25, Reuters and other outlets reported that Putin had called for an agreement between Russia and the U.S. to guarantee they would not engage in cyber-meddling in each other's elections, just as relations between the two countries reach post-Cold War lows. "(I propose)... exchanging guarantees of non-interference in each other’s internal affairs, including electoral processes, including using information and communication technologies and high-tech methods," he said. "One of the main strategic challenges of our time is the risk of a large-scale confrontation in the digital sphere."


Sept. 28 to Oct. 4


On Sept. 28, CNBC reported that, in an interview with MSNBC, House Speaker Nancy Pelosi said Trump's reported debts raise "a national security question." Pelosi's remarks come after The New York Times broke a story on Sept. 27 that Trump has $421 million of debt coming due soon and that he paid $750 in federal income taxes the year he won the presidency and during his first year in office.


On Sept. 29, Politico and other outlets reported that National Intelligence Director John Ratcliffe declassified a Russian intelligence assessment that was previously rejected by Congress as having no factual basis. In the assessment, claims are made that Clinton had personally approved an effort "to stir up a scandal" against Trump by "tying him to Putin and the Russians' hacking of the Democratic National Committee," although these claims were later found to be false.


On Sept. 29, The New York Times reported that Adam B. Schiff, Chairman of the House Intelligence Committee, has seen an uptick in Russian disinformation about mail-in ballots just as Trump has escalated his attacks on voting by mail. Although the reporting says that officials have not "explicitly linked" the disinformation campaign to the president's comments, the Russians usually focus "its disinformation efforts on existing controversies to amplify ongoing arguments."


On Oct. 1, Reuters broke a story that alleged a Russian group accused of meddling in the 2016 election had posed as an independent news outlet in order to target right-wing voters ahead of this year's election. "The website predominantly focused on U.S. politics and current events, republishing articles from conservative media and paying real Americans to write about politically-sensitive issues," the report said.


On Oct. 1, The Hill and other outlets reported that, in an interview with MSNBC, former National Security Advisor H.R. McMaster said Trump was "aiding and abetting" Putin in his effort to polarize Americans. "This sustained campaign of disruption, disinformation and denial is aided by any leader who doesn’t acknowledge it," he said. "This is why I think the president needs to be much stronger in condemning this effort to really reduce our confidence in who we are as a county."


Oct. 5 to Oct. 11


On Oct. 5, CNN and other outlets reported that the State Department had revoked the visa of Andrii Telizhenko, a former Ukrainian diplomat, who has worked with Republicans, such as Rudy Giuliani, to spread conspiracy theories about the Biden family and foreign meddling in 2016. In early September, the Treasury Department sanctioned Andrii Derkach for being an "active Russian agent," a charge they levelled against Telizhenko as well.


On Oct. 6, Bloomberg reported that Trump had authorized the declassification of documents related to the FBI's Russia investigation and the probe of Clinton's use of a private email account, part of his efforts to "cast aspersions" on his political enemies. Trump's announcement came on his first full day back in the White House after being hospitalized with COVID-19.


On Oct. 7, Reuters reported that Facebook had classified the QAnon conspiracy theory movement as dangerous and began removing the many Facebook groups and pages, as well as Instagram accounts, related to the group. "While we’ve removed QAnon content that celebrates and supports violence, we’ve seen other QAnon content tied to different forms of real world harm, including recent claims that the west coast wildfires were started by certain groups," Facebook said. "QAnon messaging changes very quickly and we see networks of supporters build an audience with one message and then quickly pivot to another."


On Oct. 9, The New York Times reported that there was growing discomfort, among the national security apparatus, that Ratcliffe was acting in a partisan manner. C.I.A. Director Gina Haspel "opposed" Ratcliffe's declassifications of documents, because it could "jeopardize spies’ ability to gather intelligence and endanger their sources."


Oct. 12 to 18


On Oct. 12, The Washington Post reported that Microsoft had taken legal steps to dismantle one of the largest botnets in an effort to thwart criminal hackers who might seek to snarl state and local computer systems used to maintain voter rolls or report on election results. "Run by Russian-speaking criminals, the botnet poses a 'theoretical but real' threat to election integrity," Tom Burt, Microsoft's Vice President of Customer Security, said. “Having just a few precincts report that they got disrupted and locked up and people couldn’t vote or their ballots can’t be counted — it’d just be pouring kerosene on the fire."


On Oct. 13, CNBC reported that Facebook will ban anti-vaccination advertisements. Previously, the company had a policy against vaccine hoaxes that were publicly identified by global health organizations, but they would still allow ads that advocate for or against legislation of government policies around vaccines, including for COVID-19.


On Oct. 14, Politico reported that the Biden campaign had lashed out against The Post's reporting on Biden's son. “This is a Russian disinformation operation,” Michael Carpenter, the former Deputy Assistant Secretary of Defense, said. “I’m very comfortable saying that.”


On Oct. 14, CNBC reported that Facebook had made the editorial decision to limit distribution of story claiming to show "smoking gun" emails related to Biden and his son, Hunter Biden. Twitter quickly follows, but backtracks a few days later, calling their move to censor the story "wrong."


On Oct. 16, NBC News broke a story that alleged U.S. authorities were investigating whether recently published emails on Biden's son, Hunter, were connected to an ongoing Russian disinformation effort targeting Biden's campaign. "The FBI seized the laptop and a hard drive through a grand jury subpoena," NBC reported. "Giuliani, who acknowledged helping bring the material to light, has in the past sought to unearth information damaging to Biden with the help of a man identified by the U.S. government as a Russian intelligence officer."


On Oct. 16, The Daily Beast broke a story that alleged former National Security Advisor John Bolton repeatedly told his staff to "actively remove themselves" from conversations and meetings with Giuliani. Bolton said he had received warnings from intelligence officials that Giuliani had been "propagating conspiracy theories" and Russian disinformation, with the purpose to undermine the election.


On Oct. 18, The New York Times reported that reporters for The Post were queasy about putting their names onto the reporting about Biden's son, Hunter. "Bruce Golding, a reporter at the Rupert Murdoch-owned tabloid since 2007, did not allow his byline to be used because he had concerns over the article’s credibility," The Times reported. "Many Post staff members questioned whether the paper had done enough to verify the authenticity of the hard drive’s contents."


Oct. 19 to 25


On Oct. 19, Politico reported that more than 50 former senior intelligence officials signed on to a letter outlining their belief that the recent disclosure of emails by The Post allegedly belonging to Biden's son, Hunter, "has all the classic earmarks of a Russian information operation." A few days prior, NBC News' reporting questioned whether the release was a foreign intelligence operation, arguing that the F.B.I. opening an investigation into this provoked many questions of whether it was credible reporting.


On Oct. 22, The Washington Post and other outlets reported that officials blamed Iran of targeting Americans voters with fake but menacing emails and warned that both Iran and Russia had obtained voter data that could be used to "endanger the upcoming elections." Ratcliffe said the voter data, which includes some data that is public and some that is commercially available, "Can be used by foreign actors to attempt to communicate false information to registered voters that they hope will cause confusion, sow chaos and undermine your confidence in American democracy."


On Oct. 22, NPR reported that Russian cyberattacks targeted a wide swath of American government networks, including those involved in the ongoing election. In an FBI bulletin, officials said, "As this recent malicious activity has been directed at … government networks, there may be some risk to elections information … However, the FBI and CISA have no evidence to date that integrity of elections data has been compromised." Later that day, The Washington Post broke a story that alleged that in one case, "a small sample of publicly available voter information was stolen."


Oct. 26 to Nov. 1


On Oct. 28, Politico broke a story that alleged Ratcliffe acted in a partisan way by going off script with his remarks on Iran, when he said they were trying "damage President Trump." Ractliffe also contrasted Iran’s actions with those of Russia, adding, “Although we have not seen the same actions from Russia, we are aware that they have obtained some voter information just as they did in 2016.”


On Oct. 28, The Wall Street Journal broke a story that alleged a computer hacker, who took over networks maintained by Hall County in Georgia, publicly released election-related files after a ransom was not paid. "A ransomware attack has occurred involving critical systems within the Hall County Government networks, including an interruption of phone services," county officials wrote. "Hall County has also enlisted the assistance of third party cyber security professionals to expedite the recovery."


On Oct. 30, CNBC reported that former Facebook Security Chief, Alex Stamos, described that election misinformation is not coming from overseas, but domestically. "But at the same time, there is election disinformation out of the White House, out of the Twitter account of the president himself and then also from theses influencers around him trying to amplify election disinformation," Stamos said. "I’m not sure we have a good solution between now and Election Day."


Nov. 2 to Nov. 8


On Nov. 2, The Guardian reported that, although officials had warned of foreign interference, the greatest threat actually came from inside the White House. "It was very difficult to see those men in suits talking about interference in the election when the White House is the one interfering with the election," Claire Wardle, the Executive Director of First Draft, said. "The media’s been obsessed with Russians under the bed, but to have the president of the United States telling people in the US that they can’t trust the results of the election — Putin could only dream of that kind of thing."


On Nov. 2, WBUR reported that, even though there was some foreign influence on the election, most of the misinformation did not actually come from Russia. "A new report finds that President Trump and the Republican Party are driving online misinformation this election, not shady actors on Facebook or Russian trolls," WBUR wrote. "Journalists don’t want to take sides or appear biased, he says, so Trump’s 'outrageous' claims are put in headlines with a fact-check saying they’re false a few paragraphs down in the story."


On Nov. 5, The New York Times broke a story that alleged that Trump and his allies are using right-wing media and internet trolls to encourage election officials to stop the legitimate counting of votes. Biden officials, in statements to The Times, decried the Trump campaign for "desperate" attempts to halt a count that seemed likely to end his presidency. "This is part of a broader misinformation campaign that involves some political theater," Bob Bauer, a senior adviser to Mr. Biden, said. "All of this is intended to create a large cloud that it is the hope of the Trump campaign that nobody can see through."


On Nov. 5, CBS News reported on the Russian government's reaction to the still-unknown result of the election. "Any uncertainty in the most powerful world economy, in one of the largest countries, has and could potentially have negative consequences for global affairs," Dmitry Peskov, a Kremlin spokesman, said. "It seems ridiculous that such a great country, the biggest democracy in the world, that their democracy is so weird and their system is so strange that it brings to the top people like these," Margarita Simonyan, the Editor-in-Chief of RT, said.


On Nov. 8, The Guardian reported that Putin and Chinese President Xi Jinping's silence, by not congratulating Biden on his presumptive win, was deafening. It is possible the "two likely losers from the defeat of Donald Trump, remained silent, perhaps waiting for the outgoing president to concede defeat."


Nov. 9 to Nov. 15


On Nov. 9, The New York Times reported that the Chinese and the Russians painted American democracy as volatile and vulnerable, likely due to Trump's fury at the result. "Russia seized that chance, while China was more restrained, perhaps reflecting cautious optimism that a President-elect Joseph R. Biden could stabilize relations with the United States," The Times wrote. "American democracy had deteriorated to the point that 'one can manipulate it, tune it, tamper with it to achieve a certain result,'" Oleg Morozov, a Russian lawmaker, said.


On Nov. 9, The Washington Post reported that when former FBI Director Andrew McCabe testifies in front of Congress on Tuesday, he would forcefully defend the bureau's decision to initiative an investigation into the Trump campaign and its connections with Russia. The Post reported he'll likely describe the decision as something of a "duty" to do so, because of how serious some of the accusations in the Steele Dossier were, including that Trump was a Russian agent.


On Nov. 10, The Conversation reported on why Russia's rigged elections look nothing like the U.S. election, because "rigged elections are organized" and "vote counts certainly don't play out in front of the country on television screens." Over many decades, the Russians have studied American democracy, especially during the Soviet Union, to attempt to figure out a way to rig an election but make it seem like it was open and free. However, "Russian elections are now state-directed performances that eliminate opposition," with random arrests of opposition leaders and even some violence.


On Nov. 13, CNN reported on a group of election officials who, in a statement, that there was no evidence of any voting system being compromised in the 2020 election, despite Trump's web of election fraud conspiracies. "The November 3rd election was the most secure in American history," the officials said. "Right now, across the country, election officials are reviewing and double checking the entire election process prior to finalizing the result."


Nov. 16 to Nov. 20


On Nov. 16, the Financial Post reported how Putin was playing chess against the West and was, supposedly, winning. With Americans so distracted by Trump over the past few years, Putin has taken more extreme measures to consolidate his power. "In August, Putin’s rival, Alexei Navalny, was poisoned," the Post wrote. "And Putin is the only major world leader who hasn’t called Joe Biden to congratulate him on his victory."


On Nov. 17, Politico reported that the U.S. intelligence community had begun "compiling an authoritative account of foreign interference and influence efforts made during this year's election — a report that could answer whether the nation's defenses held up as well as they initially appeared." The product, such as the public release, could come sometime in January, giving Biden a look at how to protect future elections from interference and prevent the chaos seen in 2016 and 2020 from ever happening again.


On Nov. 17, The Financial Times reported that Trump had fired Chris Krebs, Director of the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency, for publicly contradicting him by saying the 2020 election had been the "most secure" in history. "The recent statement by Chris Krebs on the security of the 2020 election was highly inaccurate, in that there were massive improprieties and fraud — including dead people voting," Trump, in a tweet, said. "Therefore, effective immediately, Chris Krebs has been terminated as director of the Cybersecurity and Infrastructure Security Agency."


On Nov. 17, The Washington Post broke a story that alleged Russia failed to "mount any major hacking or disinformation operations to interfere in the presidential election," because Americans and social media giants were on the lookout for posts that seemed suspicious. "This year, U.S. Cyber Command pursued a new approach to countering Russian hackers who might want to disrupt the election," The Post wrote. "In a series of operations, the military attacked their systems and fell back at random intervals to keep them off balance."


Analysis


Since the Cold War, relations between Russia and the U.S. have been flawed at best.


It is clear, just by some of the actions and statements put out by officials in both countries over the last four years, that relations would not be repaired quickly and easily.


Although Biden has pledged to be more diplomatic with Russia than Obama was, it is very unclear if Putin — who still has not congratulated Biden on his win — will be open to better dialogue, once Biden is inaugurated in Jan. In more positive terms, it is possible both superpowers may cooperate on a number of limited goals, such as the COVID-19 pandemic, to fix supply chain issues when manufacturing for the vaccine commences but I would not bet on it.


If there is one thing I have learned, by analyzing about three months of articles on the Russia investigation, it is that a free press is important to democracy. But it is even more essential that any sense of bias or war-mongering be quieted down, because The New York Times' reporting can sometimes sound like it was coming directly from the Pentagon. Additionally, it is important to note that, even though Russia clearly has been interfering in U.S. elections, it is not something unique to them: The U.S. has done the same in virtually every problematic region of the world, often with devastating consequences, as we have learned during the Cold War and even in the Middle East.


American imperialism, in all its guts and glory, simply feels irresponsible right now.


There is no question about it, American influence in world affairs has been helpful at times, but when it is nefarious, it only hurts the most vulnerable: Just take a look at Syria, likely a conflict fuelled not by a fight for democracy but for oil. And it is a similar story in Afghanistan, after 9/11, even after it was proven President George W. Bush knew they were not responsible for the terror attack, as The Washington Post outlined.


The U.S. has a president declaring he wants legal votes to be thrown out, because he will lose. If that does not tell you much about what kind of a mess the country is in right now, I am not sure what would. The Russians, on the other hand, are nipping at the heels of a full-fledged dictatorship — and even approaching Soviet Union-style leadership, with Putin — so they can not really brag about their system either, but at least they are not tilting towards a constitutional crisis.


In conclusion, both Russia and U.S. have lacked the ability to truly have decent diplomacy for many decades, often embarrassingly. It is sure as hell time they sat down and figured out what type of world they want to leave their children, because without either superpower's help, Iran will be pointing nuclear weapons at the world and Israel in 2021, even with American or Russian "interference" running rampant.
